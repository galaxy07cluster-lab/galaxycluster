Prof. Marcus Klein External Reviewer
Stanford AI Lab · 2 hours ago · on "Emergent Goal Structures"

Fascinating work on instrumental convergence. I'm curious whether your activation patching methodology controls for the possibility that the "goal-directed" circuits you identify are actually implementing heuristic shortcuts rather than genuine goal representations. In particular, did you test robustness across distribution shifts in the prompt framing? The convergence you observe at 70B might reflect emergent prompt-following rather than internal goal structures per se.

AR
Dr. Aryan Rahman Author 1 hour ago

Great question, Marcus. We did run distribution shift experiments across 12 distinct prompt framings (details in Appendix C). The circuits remained stable with cosine similarity >0.89 across framings, which we interpret as evidence against the heuristic-shortcut hypothesis. That said, you're right to be cautious — we haven't fully ruled out sophisticated prompt-following at scale. The 70B results were particularly striking because the goal representations seemed to persist even under adversarial paraphrasing.

LP
Dr. Laura Park 45 min ago

Adding to Aryan's point — there's relevant work from Anthropic's interpretability team showing that similar circuits appear in models fine-tuned with very different objectives. This corroborates the idea that these are base architecture features rather than RLHF artifacts.

Zara Ahmed, PhD Candidate
Oxford FHI · 5 hours ago · on "Value Pluralism"

The value pluralism paper raises a question I've been wrestling with: if we accept that human values are genuinely incommensurable (as Berlin argues), doesn't this imply that any AI alignment solution must be inherently political rather than technical? You'd need to resolve metaethical disputes before you could even specify what the AI should be aligned to. This seems to push the problem upstream in a way that technical alignment researchers often ignore.

AR
Dr. Aryan Rahman Author 4 hours ago

Zara, you've put your finger on exactly the tension I try to articulate in Section 4. I don't think alignment is fully "just politics," but I do think it requires a richer collaborative process than current technical approaches assume. The analogy I use is constitutional design — also a political process, but one with technical constraints and principled structure. The key is building AI systems that are capable of navigating value tradeoffs with appropriate epistemic humility rather than imposing a single value function.

Dr. Aryan Rahman

// Associate Professor · MIT BCS & CSAIL

I study the intersection of artificial intelligence, cognitive science, and ethics. My work asks both empirical questions — how do neural networks form and represent knowledge? — and normative ones: how should we build AI systems that reflect human values without imposing a single value system?

Before MIT, I was a postdoctoral fellow at DeepMind's safety team and completed my PhD at Cambridge under Professor Zoubin Ghahramani. I've been a visiting researcher at OpenAI, the Santa Fe Institute, and the Future of Humanity Institute.
